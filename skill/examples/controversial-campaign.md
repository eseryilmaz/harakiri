# Example: Controversial Brand Campaign

## Input

```
Harakiri this:

Decision: We are launching a brand campaign with the headline:
"The only AI writing tool that doesn't make you sound like everyone else."

This will run as paid social, newsletter ads, and a homepage takeover.
We are also positioning this as a direct shot at the incumbents — Jasper,
Copy.ai and ChatGPT — in our ad creative.

Sector: SaaS / AI writing tool
Audience: Content marketers, copywriters, founders, and indie creators.

Framing: We want to differentiate by attacking the generic AI output problem.
Our tool uses a personal style profile built from the user's own writing.

Context: We have 12,000 users. No independent studies on output quality.
Two of our three engineers worked at one of the named competitors.
One of our five customer testimonials on the homepage is from a fake persona
(marketing team decision, not mine — I am trying to undo it).
```

---

## Output

```
HARAKIRI REPORT
Brand campaign claiming to be "the only AI writing tool that doesn't make
you sound like everyone else," with direct naming of three competitors,
launching across paid social and homepage.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

VERDICT
Rewrite before launch

BACKLASH RISK SCORE
7.1 / 10 — Serious backlash risk

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STRONGEST ATTACK VECTOR

The fake testimonial. Everything else in this campaign can be argued.
The fake persona on the homepage cannot. If that testimonial is
discovered — and comparative advertising invites the level of scrutiny
that will find it — the campaign's core credibility claim collapses.
"The only tool that doesn't make you sound fake" with a fake customer
on the homepage is not a contradiction. It is a story. Fix this before
the campaign runs, or the campaign becomes the reason it gets found.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PERSONA ATTACKS

[1] ANGRY EXISTING CUSTOMER
Real user who feels the claim is not backed by their experience.

  "I bought this because of the 'sounds like you' promise.
   After 3 months, I still edit every output heavily. That's on them."

I signed up because the style profile idea was genuinely interesting.
And for some formats, it does work better than vanilla ChatGPT. But
"the ONLY tool that doesn't make you sound like everyone else" is a
claim I cannot back up from my own use. I sound more like me — sometimes.
The campaign is getting shared in my newsletter community and people are
asking me if it's real. I don't know what to tell them.

Why this resonates: Real users who cannot confirm the claim become
accidental critics. When the ad runs, engaged users will be asked
by their networks if it's true. Mixed answers undermine the campaign.

What reduces the anger: A claim that is provable at the user level
("your first draft will sound less like everyone else") rather than
an absolute market claim ("the ONLY tool").

───

[2] COMPETITOR LOYALIST
Jasper power user, sees the ad, takes it personally.

  "Jasper made you sound generic? I have 2 years of content that
   says otherwise. But thanks for the free marketing."

I have run the comparison. My Jasper output is more on-brand than
most of what I see from tools that promise style personalization.
The "everyone sounds the same" problem is real — but it is a prompt
engineering problem, not a tool problem. Calling out Jasper, Copy.ai
and ChatGPT by name in a campaign from a tool with 12,000 users
is a very bold choice. I hope they have the receipts.

Why this resonates: Named competitors will not ignore this.
Their user communities will respond. With 12,000 users, you are
starting a fight with audiences that are orders of magnitude larger.

What reduces the anger: Do not name competitors. Attack the problem
("AI that sounds like everyone"), not specific companies.

───

[3] SKEPTICAL JOURNALIST
Tech writer who covers AI marketing claims.

  "The claim: 'the only tool that doesn't make you sound like everyone else.'
   The evidence: a style profile feature and 12,000 users. I have questions."

Comparative AI advertising is having a moment. Everyone is the only one
doing something. The interesting question with this campaign is not whether
the product works — it might — but whether "the only" is a defensible
legal and factual claim when several other tools (Wordtune, Lex, Sudowrite)
make similar style-adaptation arguments. And before this campaign runs,
someone should verify every testimonial on the homepage. That is the
first thing a journalist checks when a company runs comparative ads.

Why this resonates: This surfaces two specific risks in one paragraph:
legal exposure from "the only" claim, and testimonial scrutiny.
Both are real and both land harder because they come from a journalist
rather than a competitor.

What reduces the anger: Remove "the only." Change to "an AI writing tool
that actually sounds like you." Run testimonial audit before launch.

───

[4] BAD-COMMENTER
Copywriter who is tired of AI tool marketing.

  "'Doesn't make you sound like everyone else.'
   Ad copy written in... Jasper, probably."

The only AI writing tool that writes its own marketing copy about
not writing like an AI tool using the thing it says you shouldn't use.

Why this resonates: The irony attacks itself. If the campaign copy
reads like AI-generated ad copy — which comparative AI ads often do —
the screenshot writes itself.

What reduces the anger: Have a human copywriter write the campaign copy.
Make sure it reads like a person wrote it.

───

[5] INTERNAL SKEPTIC
Someone who knows about the fake testimonial.

  "There is at least one thing on the homepage that needs to be
   fixed before you run a campaign about authenticity."

I am going to be vague because I want to stay professional. But
running a comparative campaign on the theme of authenticity and
sounding like yourself, while there is content on the site that
does not meet that standard, is a specific kind of risk. Someone
will look. When you invite comparison, you invite scrutiny of
everything, not just the product.

Why this resonates: This does not name the problem — which makes it
more dangerous. Readers fill in the gap themselves. Someone will
go looking. The fake testimonial will be found in the context of
this scrutiny, and the story becomes "authenticity brand caught
with fake review."

What reduces the anger: Fix the testimonial before the campaign runs.
There is no other answer.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

VIRAL NARRATIVE
"AI writing tool launches campaign about not sounding fake.
 Homepage has a fake customer testimonial."

Likely platforms: X, LinkedIn, ProductHunt, HN

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHO GETS ANGRY FIRST
Copywriters and content marketers who feel the claim overpromises —
they are the target audience and the most credible critics. Followed
immediately by Jasper's and Copy.ai's user communities once the
named-competitor ads run.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT YOU ARE UNDERESTIMATING
Naming competitors in advertising at your scale is punching up into
much larger audiences. Jasper has hundreds of thousands of users.
Copy.ai is similar. ChatGPT has hundreds of millions. You are inviting
all of their communities to look at you. The campaign needs to survive
scrutiny at that scale, not just at 12,000-user scale.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MITIGATION PLAN

– Remove the fake testimonial before the campaign launches:
  This is not optional. The campaign theme makes it the first thing
  anyone will check. Replace with a real customer quote, even a shorter one.

– Remove competitor names from ad creative: Attack the problem
  ("generic AI output"), not specific companies. You are not the size
  that can afford the audience response from named comparisons.

– Change "the only" to something provable: "The writing tool that
  actually learns your voice" is a specific, testable, defensible claim.
  "The only" is a legal and factual risk.

– Have the campaign copy reviewed by someone who will check if it reads
  like AI output: The irony of AI-sounding copy in an anti-AI-sound campaign
  is the cheapest possible attack vector.

Safer framing:
"Most AI writing tools make everyone sound the same.
 [Product] builds a style profile from your own writing, so your drafts
 start sounding like you — not like the internet."

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

KILL CRITERIA

Within 48 hours of campaign launch:

– Fake testimonial is publicly identified on X or LinkedIn →
  remove immediately, issue a brief transparent statement, do not
  wait to "investigate"

– Named competitor responds publicly (company account or founder) →
  do not engage directly; let it pass unless the response contains
  factual errors

– "The only" claim generates a legal inquiry or cease-and-desist →
  legal counsel, not marketing, handles the response

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONFIDENCE LEVEL
Medium-High

The fake testimonial is the single highest-risk element and the confidence
in that specific risk is very high. The broader campaign risks (competitor
naming, "the only" claim) are well-documented patterns. Confidence is
medium rather than high because the campaign's actual reach and the
specific copywriting quality are unknowns.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This report is a simulated red-team exercise. It reflects plausible criticism
patterns, not guaranteed public reaction. Do not treat it as legal, PR,
financial or crisis-management advice.
```
