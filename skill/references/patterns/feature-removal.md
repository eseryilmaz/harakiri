# Feature Removal — Calibration Patterns

This file contains synthetic complaint patterns for tone calibration. The examples are illustrative, not real customer quotes. Do not reproduce them verbatim in generated reports.

---

## Pattern 1: Dependency Betrayal

**Emotional trigger:** Helplessness. Users built workflows around the feature and are now stranded.

**Complaint angle:** The feature was not a convenience — it was a foundation. Removing it does not inconvenience users; it breaks something they built on top of it.

**Synthetic calibration phrases:**
- "I built my entire weekly review process around this. Six months of automation, gone."
- "You don't remove a feature people depend on. You find a way to replace it."
- "I chose this product because of this feature. Removing it means I chose the wrong product."

**Likely platforms:** Reddit, support forums, Hacker News

**Who amplifies it:** Developers, power users, anyone who built scripts or workflows on top of the feature

**What reduces the anger:** A documented migration path; a replacement feature with equivalent capability; advance notice long enough to rebuild workflows

---

## Pattern 2: Data Hostage

**Emotional trigger:** Violation. Users feel their own data is being held behind a paywall.

**Complaint angle:** The removal affects how users access or export data they created. Framed not as a feature loss but as a change in the ownership relationship between the user and their content.

**Synthetic calibration phrases:**
- "It's my data. I should be able to export it in any format I want without paying extra."
- "They're not removing a feature. They're putting my own content behind a paywall."
- "When you gate data export, the question becomes: whose data is it, really?"

**Likely platforms:** Hacker News, Reddit (r/privacy, r/selfhosted), X

**Who amplifies it:** Privacy advocates, developers, self-hosting communities, anyone who cares about data portability as a principle

**What reduces the anger:** A one-time free export option before the paywall activates; a clear statement that user data ownership is unaffected

---

## Pattern 3: Bait-and-Switch

**Emotional trigger:** Betrayal. The free feature was the reason they chose the product.

**Complaint angle:** The feature was explicitly listed as free during acquisition. Removing it retroactively changes the terms under which the commitment was made.

**Synthetic calibration phrases:**
- "It was listed as free when I signed up. I have the screenshot."
- "This is the definition of bait-and-switch. Free to get you in, paid to keep using it."
- "They advertised the free tier. The free tier was why I chose them over the alternative."

**Likely platforms:** X, Reddit, review sites

**Who amplifies it:** Anyone who signed up specifically for the removed feature; anyone with access to old marketing materials or launch posts

**What reduces the anger:** Grandfathering users who signed up under the prior terms; explicit acknowledgment of the prior promise in the announcement

---

## Pattern 4: Enshittification Signal

**Emotional trigger:** Cynicism. The removal is read as the opening move in a monetization sequence.

**Complaint angle:** Users interpret the removal not as a feature decision but as a deliberate step in a larger pattern — first make it free to build dependency, then charge for it once leaving is costly.

**Synthetic calibration phrases:**
- "They removed it to sell it back to us as a premium feature. Classic."
- "Whatever you rely on, they will eventually charge for. This is just the current example."
- "Free feature today, Pro feature tomorrow, gone entirely next year."

**Likely platforms:** Hacker News, X, Reddit

**Who amplifies it:** Tech-savvy users who recognize the pattern; anyone who has seen it before at other companies

**What reduces the anger:** Hard to counter once the framing is established. Announcing the decision alongside a feature that expands the free tier reduces the narrative.

---

## Pattern 5: Consent Violation

**Emotional trigger:** Powerlessness. The change happened without warning or input.

**Complaint angle:** Users feel the product changed under their feet. The complaint is not just about the feature — it is about the process. Nobody asked, nobody was warned, and there is no way back.

**Synthetic calibration phrases:**
- "Nobody asked for this. Not one person on the forum was asking for this."
- "They removed it and called it a product improvement. For whom?"
- "I wasn't consulted. I was informed. There's a difference."

**Likely platforms:** Reddit, community forums, X

**Who amplifies it:** Community-engaged users who feel entitled to input on product decisions; anyone who follows the product closely

**What reduces the anger:** A comment period before the change; a clear explanation of the user problem the removal solves (if any); a rollback option or transition period
